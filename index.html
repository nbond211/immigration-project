Refugees and Immigration to the United States in the Early 20th Century

By Peyton K. Tiroff and Nicholas K. Bond

Immigration has played a crucial role in the development of the United States as a nation. Historically, the concept of a “melting pot “has been used as a metaphor for the blending of nationalities, cultures and ethnicities that exist in the heterogeneous society of the United States as a result of diverse immigration. America’s history of diversity coexists with a track record of xenophobia. The Immigration Act of 1924 limited the annual number of immigrants who could be admitted from any country to 2% of the number of people from that country who were already living in the United States as of the 1890 census. The law was created with the intention of reducing immigration from Southern and Eastern Europe and according to the U.S. Department of State Office of the Historian the purpose of the act was "to preserve the ideal of American homogeneity". 1
